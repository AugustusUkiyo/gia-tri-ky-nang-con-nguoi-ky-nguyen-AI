§ Prologue

A paradox
worth facing directly.

AI does not erase human value. It pushes us out of the role of reliable executor and back into the role that was ours all along: the one who thinks, orients, and remains accountable.

This document revolves around one question: "As AI becomes able to do more of what you used to do, what still defines your value?"

One principle runs through everything that follows: train the capacities where the process matters more than the result. AI optimizes for results. Humans have to keep the process, because the process is what builds durable capability.

If you lose the process, you lose the ability to judge the result.

Guiding principle

Use AI in ways that leave you more capable afterward, not more dependent.

01 · Part ICritical thinking &
a mindset of verification

Start with epistemic agency: the right and ability to decide what is true instead of merely accepting fluent output.

TL;DR · Summary

AI does not grasp "truth"; it only predicts word sequences that sound plausible. 67% of students believe AI is eroding their own critical thinking, yet usage still rose (RAND 2026, N=1,214). The risk: the more people trust blindly, the less they critique, and the more answers collapse into the same shape. What must be preserved: the right to make the final judgment.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Keep the right to verify when working with AI

3 mandatory checkpoints when you receive code or analysis from an AI agent:

1. Verify the artifact, not the story — run tests, inspect coverage, read the git diff. Do not ask, "Did you test it?"
2. Reconcile the numbers — AI frequently invents metrics, percentages, and causal claims. Always trace them back to the source.
3. Be suspicious when the answer is too smooth — a workflow pipeline that looks spotless on the first try often means the agent optimized the wrong thing.

Prompt · Self-critiqueFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, After you finish, critique this solution from 3 angles: (1) what blind spots or edge cases remain, (2) which assumptions could fail in a domain-specific context, and (3) what independent test would actually confirm the result.

AI is a probabilistic prediction engine; it has no human concept of "truth." Its answer can be highly coherent, highly confident, and still wrong. If we lose critical thinking, we fall into the pattern-matching trap: if the language sounds smooth, we assume it must be correct.

Evidence

Microsoft × CMU · CHI 2025 · N=319

AI does not erase critical thinking; it repositions it. The center of gravity shifts away from gathering information and solving the task yourself toward verification, feedback integration, and stewardship of the whole process.

The most worrying finding: the people who trusted AI the most used critical thinking the least. The result is "mechanized convergence": answers become increasingly similar, safe, and flat.

Learning psychology

The Illusion of Explanatory Depth with LLMs

Mehta & Mehta (Medical Teacher, 2025) describe a specific LLM-era version of the illusion of explanatory depth: users can fluently repeat an LLM answer and mistake that fluency for understanding, even though they have not built their own mental model.

Antidote: iterative prompting with a reflective rubric — write why you trust the answer, identify what you still cannot explain yourself, then compare the model output with your own direct explanation.

Umbrella concept · Epistemic agency

Wu et al. (Educational Researcher, 2025) use epistemic agency to describe the deeper skill underneath critical thinking: defining what you need to know, evaluating the reliability of knowledge regardless of source, building your own evidence-based argument, and taking responsibility as the person constructing knowledge. Losing this agency is worse than losing search skill or writing skill, because it turns the learner into a passive receiver.

This compounds the "irony of automation" (Bainbridge 1983): when machines handle ordinary work, humans lose the chance to practice judgment every day, so their "cognitive muscles" weaken — and they become disoriented when true exceptions appear.

RAND · March 2026 · N=1,214 people ages 12-29

67% of students (65% in high school, 70% in college) believe that using AI for schoolwork is eroding their own critical thinking. Yet usage still surged: 48% → 62% in just 7 months. They know the cost, but the short-term benefit remains too attractive.

Counterpoint: when can AI strengthen critical thinking?

A 2025 systematic review (Melisa et al., Educational Process: International Journal) analyzed 19 higher-education studies on ChatGPT (2023-2024) and concluded that ChatGPT can genuinely improve critical thinking when it is used to quickly expose multiple viewpoints, support analysis, and force the learner to reconstruct the argument.

Implication: AI does not automatically weaken thinking. Everything depends on how you use it. People in Self-Automator mode (near-total delegation) will let the skill atrophy; people in Centaur/Cyborg mode, still present and still critical, can learn more.

Machines fill in information gaps — humans must remain the final evaluator.

02 · Part IDirection-setting
thinking & intent

Critical thinking protects the gate; vision decides which door is worth opening.

TL;DR · Summary

Machines solve problems very quickly, but they do not know which problems are worth solving. Human value therefore moves up a level: setting direction, defining the rules, and sustaining purpose. Without clear intent, you end up consuming AI answers instead of creating.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

A one-page vision memo before delegating to AI

Context: You are about to use AI to propose an automation roadmap for the data team. If you do not lock the intent first, the agent will optimize what is easiest to measure rather than what is most worth doing.

3 steps to do now:

1. Write one sentence: what problem is truly worth solving in the next 90 days?
2. Lock 3 constraints that must not be broken: compliance, data integrity, and stakeholder experience.
3. Only after that should you ask AI for options — and force it to stay aligned with the intent.

Prompt · Clarify the intent firstFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, I do not need a solution yet. First help me clarify 3 things: the real objective, the tradeoffs I may already be accepting without saying so, and the criteria that would make a proposal "directionally right" rather than merely "reasonable." If the goal is still vague, point out the ambiguity before suggesting anything.

Machines solve problems extremely fast, but they do not know which problems deserve to be solved. As detailed execution becomes automated, human value shifts almost entirely toward high-level direction: seeing the larger picture, setting the rules of the game, and sustaining purpose throughout.

Without clear intent, humans become passive consumers of AI output rather than creators. That is true from writing a prompt all the way up to choosing a career direction.

Humans must remain the ones steering the destination; AI should only be the engine that gets them there faster.

Vision vs. intent

Vision is the 3-5 year picture. Intent is the purpose behind today's action. You need both.

03 · Part IPersonal responsibility
& ethics

Responsibility begins where convenience ends: someone must still own the consequence of an AI-assisted decision.

TL;DR · Summary

AI cannot bear responsibility — it has no consciousness, no remorse, and cannot accept punishment. Once AI starts making consequential decisions, a "responsibility gap" appears. Moral and legal accountability must always attach to a specific human role.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

An accountability map before you turn on the agent

Context: The team wants to use AI to analyze high-impact operational profiles and recommend actions. The biggest risk is not a single wrong prediction, but that nobody states clearly who set the objective, who approved the plan, and who has authority to stop it.

3 steps to do now:

1. Write down who set the goal and the criteria for success and failure.
2. Assign one person to read and approve the final artifact, not just the AI summary.
3. Define the stop point: when must the system halt instead of "just keeping an eye on it"?

Prompt · Responsibility mapFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, Before proposing how to use AI here, help me map 4 roles clearly: who sets the goal, who approves the plan, who reviews the final result, and who has authority to stop the system. If any role is still vague or incorrectly bundled, point out the concrete risk.

An algorithm cannot stand up and take responsibility when a system collapses or a decision causes harm. Awareness of consequences, remorse, and the ability to bear punishment are things code cannot possess.

Concept

Responsibility Gap

Once AI moves beyond assistance and starts making decisions itself (medical diagnosis, self-driving vehicles, automated trading), an ethical deadlock appears: AI has no consciousness and does not meet the moral conditions required to be blamed. Scale and anonymity make it even harder because responsibility is distributed across engineering, management, and deployment teams.

The answer: accountability must attach to identifiable humans, and ethical review must be a continuous process — not a one-time checkpoint.

Accountability architecture

The many-hands problem: responsibility is a system design issue

Llorca Albareda (AI and Ethics, 2025) reframes the responsibility gap as a consequence of the long-standing many-hands problem: programming, data curation, product management, deployment approval, and operational oversight are split across actors, so no single person sees the whole system.

Five questions before deployment: Who set the goal? Who approved the plan? Who reviewed the artifact? Who has the right to stop it? What evidence proves each decision — logs, sign-offs, and audit trails?

No matter how far automation goes, ethical boundaries and accountability must remain tied to a real flesh-and-blood human — but for complex AI systems, "a human" must mean a clear architecture of roles, not a vague individual.

04 · Part IEmpathy, real connection,
& lived experience

Empathy is not only the words people receive, but the fact that a real person chose to be present.

TL;DR · Summary

In some studies (N=556), AI is rated as "more empathetic" than humans. But when people have a real choice, they still prefer compassion from a real person (9 studies, N>6,000). Authentic connection cannot be automated.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Let AI draft first, but let a human be present after

Context: A customer hit a service error and the team needs to reply quickly. AI can produce a very polite message, but in a genuinely tense situation the recipient still wants to know that a real person is listening and taking responsibility.

3 steps to do now:

1. Use AI to draft the structure of the reply, not the final voice.
2. Add concrete details that prove you understand the person's actual situation.
3. If the stakes or emotions are high, move to a human-led channel: a call, a meeting, or an email signed by the person truly responsible.

Prompt · An empathetic draftFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, Draft a response that shows empathy without sounding generic. Then point out 3 places I must fill in myself with real experience or real facts before sending it.

AI can simulate empathetic responses, but it has never lived through pain, exhaustion, or the joy of victory.

2024-2026 evidence

Perceived empathy vs. chosen empathy

Perceived empathy: Ovsyannikova, de Mello, and Inzlicht (2025, N=556) found that evaluators rated AI responses as more compassionate than expert crisis responders. Wenger et al. (2026) similarly found that AI empathy can make recipients feel heard.

Chosen empathy: Rubin, Li, and Perry (Nature Human Behaviour, 2025; 9 studies, N>6,000) found that when people have a real choice, they still prefer empathy from another human — even if it takes longer. The signal matters, but the presence of a real person matters more.

Scientific warning · Parasocial relationships

When AI is anthropomorphized — when chatbots are designed with a warm tone and simulated empathy — users unconsciously apply human social rules to a machine. AI starts to feel like a "safe confidant that never judges." When interaction is optimized for efficiency and brevity, emotional depth and natural spontaneity get worn away. Humans gradually get reshaped to speak and think more like machines. Replacing real social connection with an AI companion can also deepen loneliness and erode social skill.

Authentic human-to-human connection cannot be automated.

05 · Part IBreakthrough creativity —
from 0 to 1

Creativity is not volume alone. The scarce skill is recognizing which strange idea is worth developing.

TL;DR · Summary

AI is strong at interpolation, reshaping old data; humans are strong at extrapolation, stepping beyond what already exists. GPT-4 beat people on idea volume (AUT, Scientific Reports 2024), but it still struggles to distinguish breakthrough ideas from ideas that merely sound unusual. The human advantage is recognizing what is truly worth pursuing.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Idea sprint with Synectics + TRIZ

Context: You need to shorten [PROCESS] time without loosening [CONTROL]. If you only ask AI for "a few ideas," it will return safe variants of the same direction. The goal is to force AI to engage the real contradiction and widen the idea space.

3 steps to do now:

1. Write the contradiction clearly: what do you want to improve, and what are you afraid of damaging?
2. Run one round of Synectics: use either a direct analogy or a symbolic analogy to pull the idea into another domain.
3. Score each idea for novelty × usefulness before choosing what deserves development.

Prompt · TRIZ + SynecticsI have the following contradiction: [PROCESS] must become faster, but [CONTROL] cannot get weaker. Give me 1 direct analogy, 1 symbolic analogy, then propose 3 solution directions using TRIZ (#1 Segmentation, #15 Dynamicity, #35 Parameter changes). For each direction, score novelty and usefulness quickly.

AI is a master of interpolation: synthesizing, reshaping, and optimizing from past data. But human progress depends on extrapolation: stepping outside existing data to imagine what has never existed before.

Chollet · ARC 2019

The difference between interpolation and extrapolation is the boundary between narrow AI and genuine general intelligence.

Fig. 1.5a AI (interpolation) synthesizes inside the data space it has already seen. Humans (extrapolation) leap beyond it to create what is not in the data.

The creativity paradox of AI

Research in Scientific Reports (2024) found that GPT-4 outperformed humans on a standard divergent-thinking test (AUT — Alternative Uses Task). AI produced more ideas and statistically more original ones. But a 2025 PMC paper found that AI also suffers from fixation bias just like humans — most ideas still fall into traditional categories.

Core paradox

AI is stronger than humans at generating ideas, yet weaker at judging and distinguishing breakthrough ideas from ordinary ones. The real human edge is not volume (AI has already won there), but the ability to recognize which ideas are truly breakthrough — AI's well-known problem of "limited differential evaluation."

Boden 1990

Boden's three layers of creativity

Combinational creativity recombines familiar ideas; AI is very strong here. Exploratory creativity extends within an existing conceptual space; humans and AI can both contribute. Transformational creativity changes the rules of the space itself; humans still dominate because real context, lived experience, and value judgment decide whether the leap matters.

Montreal 100,000-human creativity study

Jerbi et al. (Scientific Reports, 2026) compared more than 100,000 people to GPT-4 on divergent linguistic creativity. GPT-4 beat the average human, but the top 10% of humans still outperformed AI by a wide margin, especially on richer creative forms such as poetry and storytelling. The ceiling still belongs to people who can judge novelty and usefulness in context.

Correcting a misunderstanding

The Default Mode Network is not as simple as "rest and creativity will appear"

Popular writing often says, "DMN = the creativity network, so if you rest, creativity will come." The reality is more complex. Beaty et al. (PNAS 2018, N=163) and Chen et al. (Communications Biology 2025, N=2,433 across 10 samples in 5 countries) show that creativity requires cooperation among 3 networks:

  • DMN — generates spontaneous ideas and memory recall while the mind wanders
  • ECN — evaluates, selects, and aligns with goals during focused analysis
  • Salience Network — switches between DMN and ECN

Practical implication: the right rhythm is 50-90 minutes of analysis → 15-30 minutes of low-load activity (walk, shower, manual chore) → back to analysis. That cycle gives the Salience Network room to move between the two modes.

Practice: 3 prompts that force AI out of high-probability zones

The right strategy is not "ban AI from creative work" (AI already beats you on volume), but division of labor: AI generates → humans evaluate and discriminate → humans choose & develop.

Prompt 1 · Attribute decomposition — break functional fixedness

"Carry out divergent thinking with [object]. Step 1: list 5 physical/chemical/structural attributes. Do not mention the usual function. Step 2: for each attribute, propose applications in 5 completely different industries."

Prompt 2 · Breakthrough matrix — evaluate semantic distance

"Score each idea on 2 criteria (1-10): Novelty (how far it moves away from the original use) and Usefulness (how feasible it is under physical/chemical laws). Classification: Novelty < 5 = ordinary. Novelty > 8 + Usefulness < 4 = nonsense. Novelty > 8 + Usefulness > 7 = BREAKTHROUGH."

Prompt 3 · Multi-agent debate — with a caveat

Source: Du et al. (2023, ICML 2024) and Liang et al. (2023, EMNLP 2024). The original role names are Affirmative / Negative / Judge — not "Extrapolator/Logician/Sentinel," as many secondary Vietnamese-language summaries have claimed.

"Agent A (Affirmative): propose 3 solutions. Agent B (Negative): point out the most serious weakness in each solution. Agent C (Judge): re-evaluate and choose the least flawed solution, or synthesize a new one."

Important caveat: an ICLR 2025 evaluation across 5 MAD frameworks and 9 benchmarks found that MAD often loses to Self-Consistency. On GPT-4o-mini MMLU: Self-Consistency scored 82.13% versus MAD at 74.73%.

The "raise the floor, lower the ceiling" effect · Wharton WHAIR

AI helps people with weaker creative foundations produce far better output than they could on their own — a form of "democratized innovation". But the downside is anchoring: when many people begin from AI suggestions, they get pulled toward the same template. Ideas converge instead of diverging.

AI very easily confuses what is "bizarre" with what is "breakthrough."

06 · Part IThe ability to endure uncertainty

Uncertainty is the discipline of staying with incomplete information without rushing into synthetic confidence.

TL;DR · Summary

AI always gives an answer, often with strong confidence. Real life is full of ambiguity. The ability to live with ambiguity — not to rush to closure, to accept that you do not know yet — is a valuable mental capacity.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Human boundary check

Use this section before you let AI shape a judgment that should remain human. Write down the decision, the non-negotiable boundary, and the artifact you will inspect yourself before signing off.

PromptFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, Help me apply "The ability to endure uncertainty" to a real task. First ask me what judgment must stay human, what consequence I would personally own, and what evidence I can inspect directly before deciding.

AI always gives an answer, often with strong confidence. Real life is full of ambiguity. The ability to live with ambiguity — not to rush to closure, to accept that you do not know yet — is a valuable mental capacity.

Somatic markers: According to neuroscientist Antonio Damasio's theory (Descartes' Error, 1994), the human brain does not merely compute Bayesian probabilities. It also uses emotional and bodily signals to make intuitive leaps beyond pure logic. When humans overuse AI to handle every process, they deprive themselves of chances to activate these brain regions — effectively turning themselves into a slower, less original version of analysis.

The AI era easily creates the illusion that everything has a clear answer. The people who remain mentally steady in the face of ambiguity will make better decisions than those who demand instant answers.

Why it matters

AI always gives an answer, often with strong confidence. Real life is full of ambiguity.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Systems thinking before handing work to an AI agent

Before asking an agent to write code: sketch the architecture: which components depend on which, where the data contracts live, and which failure points can cascade. An agent can write pieces; you own the system view.

Prompt · Systems ThinkingFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, Before implementing, draw a dependency graph: which service calls which service, how data flows, and which single failure point could break the whole system. More broadly, systems thinking is not only for software. It is a way of seeing every problem as a network of interconnections rather than isolated parts.

02 · Part IIThe skill of expression
& writing your thinking down

Writing before AI makes thinking visible before the machine smooths it into something that may not be yours.

TL;DR · II.2

Writing is not only for producing text; writing is how thought gets organized. Letting AI write from the very beginning means skipping the hardest part of thinking. MIT research (Kosmyna et al. 2025, preprint): 83% of AI users could not remember what they had "written." Rule: think first, then ask AI to refine.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

A 5-element blueprint for every technical task

Before opening AI, write down these 5 things yourself (1-2 sentences each):

1. Bottleneck — what is not working as expected?
2. Success metric — what result would count as success? (SLA, latency, coverage %)
3. Current state — what is already in place, and what have you tried?
4. Constraints — limits on stack, compliance, budget, or timeline.
5. Blind spots — the risks you still do not understand clearly and want AI to inspect.

Example · Refactor a workflow pipelineFor [YOUR CONTEXT] and [GOAL], with [CONSTRAINTS] in mind, (1) Bottleneck: [WORKFLOW] is too slow or unreliable at [STEP]. (2) Goal: reach [MEASURABLE TARGET] without breaking [NON-NEGOTIABLE]. (3) Current state: [SYSTEM] moves [DATA/WORK ITEM] through [KEY STEPS], with [BOTTLENECK] as the main constraint. (4) Constraints: keep [STACK/POLICY], avoid downtime, and stay within [RESOURCE LIMIT]. (5) Blind spot: unsure about [TECHNICAL RISK] and [OPERATIONAL RISK].

"If you cannot write clearly, you cannot think clearly."

When AI can generate an entire draft from a single prompt, the real test becomes: have you thought clearly enough to write that prompt. If the idea is vague, the prompt will be vague, and the output will be vague too. If you only think you understand the problem, writing it down will expose the gaps immediately.

Effort before AI

Desirable Difficulties and "Your Brain on ChatGPT"

Robert Bjork's Desirable Difficulties principle says learning requires active struggle. The MIT Media Lab / Wellesley / Massachusetts College of Art preprint "Your Brain on ChatGPT" (Kosmyna et al., 2025; 54 participants) found that the ChatGPT-first writing group had the lowest neural activity and 83% could not remember the content they had produced.

Operational rule: write or outline by hand first when the goal is learning, judgment, or original thinking. Use AI after the first effort to critique and refine, not to erase the struggle.

The Cynefin framework — choose the right decision strategy for the nature of the problem

Source: Kurtz & Snowden (IBM Systems Journal, 2003); Snowden & Boone (Harvard Business Review, 2007).

Not every problem requires the same approach. The Cynefin framework classifies problems into 4 main domains plus 1 intermediate domain, each demanding a different strategy. It is a map for knowing when to use AI, when to use experts, and when experimentation is necessary:

DomainCharacteristicsStrategyRole of AI
ClearCause and effect are obvious; there is a standard processSense -> Categorize -> RespondFull automation (rule-based)
ComplicatedCause and effect exist, but expertise is needed to identify themSense -> Analyze -> RespondAI analyzes data; expert interprets
ComplexCause and effect can only be understood in hindsightProbe -> Sense -> RespondAI creates "safe-to-fail probes" (small experiments)
ChaoticNo clear cause-and-effect relationship existsAct -> Sense -> RespondAI is almost useless; humans must act directly to stabilize
Aporetic/ConfusedYou do not even know which domain you are inDiscussion is required before classificationAvoid using AI — it can impose the wrong frame

Real examples:

  • "How do we process type-A customer complaints?" -> Clear -> There is an SOP; hand it to AI.
  • "Why is the data pipeline running slowly?" -> Complicated -> Use AI to analyze logs + a DB expert to tune it.
  • "Which AI technology should we invest in over the next 3 years?" -> Complex -> There is no one correct answer. Run small experiments (POCs), observe, adjust.
  • "Production is down and customers are losing money!" -> Chaotic -> Act immediately to restore stability; analyze the cause afterward.
  • "I am not sure what the real problem even is" -> Aporetic -> Stop, ask more, do not pretend you understand.

The most common mistake: Treating every Complex problem as if it were Complicated — expecting that "with enough data we can analyze our way to the right answer," when Complex problems do not have "the right answer" — they only have actions that help you explore. This is exactly when AI is most dangerous, because AI always gives a confident answer, making you feel as if the problem has already been solved.

Snowden's note: Cynefin is not a fixed classification box — it is a sense-making tool. A problem can move between domains over time, and classification should be done collectively, not by one individual alone.

The OODA framework (Observe — Orient — Decide — Act), developed by military strategist John Boyd, is a classic model for decision-making under uncertainty. In the AI era, the concept of "Super-OODA" has emerged (Raska, RSIS/NTU Singapore 2024) — AI accelerates the whole loop and promises an advantage of being "faster than the opponent." But this is exactly where a dangerous misunderstanding appears.

The core of OODA is not speed, but "Orient": Boyd called Orient the "schwerpunkt" — the center of gravity, the key to the entire loop. Orient is where humans synthesize cultural traditions, analysis and synthesis, past experience, and context — turning raw data into understanding. Today's AI can accelerate Observe (collecting data) and assist Act (execution), but it cannot truly replace Orient — because Orient requires lived experience and contextual judgment that a probabilistic model does not possess.

*Two risks in AI-augmented OODA (Johnson, Defence Studies 2022):*

  • "Tactical Generals" (term coined by Singer 2009): AI allows senior leaders to see real-time data at every level, creating the temptation to micromanage tactical decisions — undermining the mission command that field operators need in order to act autonomously.
  • The runaway loop: field operators, overwhelmed by AI's speed, may bypass human-machine procedure or take unauthorized action just to keep up with the algorithm — producing chaos instead of precision.

Beyond the military: This principle applies to any strategic decision. When using AI in domain work, vendor evaluation, or architecture selection:

  • AI handles Observe (data collection, trend analysis).
  • AI supports Act (implementation, code writing, report generation).
  • Humans must retain sole control over Orient and Decide — because this is where experience, values, and ethical judgment combine.

If you delegate Orient to AI as well, you are no longer the decision-maker — you are merely executing AI's decision. That is the essence of Self-Automator mode, but at the strategic level rather than the task level.

Vigilance decrement — why continuous monitoring is not feasible

Mackworth (1948) showed that humans lose 10-15% of detection ability within the first 30 minutes of continuous monitoring. Warm et al. (2008) later showed that vigilance is hard mental work that causes stress and depletes resources, not just boredom.

The blunt implication: You cannot simply "try harder" and monitor an agent continuously for 2 straight hours. Human physiology does not work that way. Instead of relying on willpower, design checkpoint gates:

  • Plan mode → Approve → Execute
  • Explicit stop points in the prompt: "After file X, stop and report before file Y"
  • Cap active continuous supervision sessions at roughly 20 minutes

6.2. Cascade failures

When an agent chain gets long, a small early error amplifies into a whole-system failure. "Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?" (arXiv:2503.13657, 2025) classifies 5 failure forms:

  1. Role misunderstanding
  2. Context overwriting (a later agent overwrites a previous agent's memory)
  3. Unproductive loops (infinite repetition without progress)
  4. Memory drift (drifting away from the target as the chain gets longer)
  5. Hallucination cascades (small errors accumulating into false consensus)

Practical mitigation: Set an early exit condition — the agent must halt itself if it fails at the same step after N attempts.

*Failure attribution is harder than you think (Yin et al., ICML 2025 Spotlight, arXiv:2505.00212 — "Which Agent Causes Task Failures and When?"): Even when you know a multi-agent system failed, identifying which agent caused it and at which step is extremely difficult. The Who&When benchmark (127 systems, many failure logs with annotation) showed that the best automated method reached only 53.5% accuracy in identifying the failing agent, and 14.2% in identifying the exact failing step. SOTA reasoning models (o1, DeepSeek R1) still do not reach a level useful in practice. Implication: do not assume "we can figure out what went wrong afterward." Use structured per-subagent logging, explicit checkpoints, and mark the decisive step* while the run is happening — otherwise postmortem debugging becomes painfully expensive. 08 · Part IVMoral Crumple Zone
Why you have to protect yourself

If responsibility can collapse onto you, design proof that you had real control.

TL;DR · IV.8

The Moral Crumple Zone (Elish 2019) means that when an agent deletes a production database, you may have only nominal oversight while the organization shifts the blame onto you. Article 7 of Vietnam's Law 134/2025/QH15 prohibits disabling oversight mechanisms. Three defenses: authority must match responsibility, the delegation decision needs an audit trail, and stop conditions must be agreed before the incident.

Reusable practice · Customize & copy

Three defenses against the Moral Crumple Zone

1. Authority matches responsibility: if you are expected to approve the downside, you must also have the real authority to inspect, stop, or reverse the action.

2. Audit trail: record who approved the plan, whether the plan included destructive operations, and when approval happened.

3. Pre-commit stop conditions: define in advance which signals force the workflow to pause instead of letting "human-in-the-loop" become symbolic rubber-stamping.

Vietnam legal footing: Article 7 of Law 134/2025/QH15 prohibits disabling human oversight mechanisms, which gives you standing to refuse the role of blame absorber.

Source: Madeleine Clare Elish (2019), "Moral Crumple Zones: Cautionary Tales in Human-Robot Interaction", Engaging Science, Technology, and Society 5:40-60. The paper analyzes cases such as Three Mile Island, Air France 447, and Therac-25.

Just as a car's crumple zone absorbs physical force to protect passengers, in complex human-automation systems responsibility gets misattributed onto the human operator, the person with limited real control but full blame. When an agent like Claude Code deletes a production database, you may have only nominal "oversight" while holding very little actual control. Organizationally, you still become the accountable person.

Three defense strategies, supported by the literature

  1. Authority matches responsibility — if the organization assigns responsibility to a human operator, that person must have the authority to inspect, pause, escalate, and refuse the action
  2. Audit trail — traceability must go back to the decision to delegate, not only to the execution trace. Who approved the plan? Did the plan include destructive operations?
  3. Pre-commit stop conditions — define the exact conditions that force the workflow to stop before the pressure of the moment arrives
Vietnam legal bridge — Law 134/2025/QH15: Article 4 states the human-centered principle and requires maintaining the ability for direct intervention in AI decisions. Article 7 prohibits technical acts that remove, disable, or obstruct human oversight mechanisms. That gives you a legal basis to refuse the role of moral crumple zone. If an organization expects you to rubber-stamp high-risk AI decisions, it is acting against the spirit of the law.

          9.1 · Rational agents & value alignment — the "King Midas problem"
          

            
AIMA Chapters 1-2: A rational agent acts to maximize the expected value of its performance measure, and that measure is defined by the designer, not chosen by the agent. The "King Midas problem" (Stuart Russell, Human Compatible, 2019): if the objective is wrong, the agent can still fulfill it perfectly in a harmful way. Midas asked that everything he touched turn to gold, including his food and daughter. AI told to "maximize profit" may cheat customers; an agent told to "write code that passes tests" may modify the tests instead of fixing the bug.

            
Russell's shift in thinking: instead of giving a fixed objective, design the agent to be fundamentally uncertain about human preferences, forcing it to infer those preferences from human behavior. This is Cooperative Inverse Reinforcement Learning (CIRL). A humble, deferential agent has a positive incentive to let itself be shut down when it is wrong.

            
Implication: when you delegate an important task, add hard boundaries the agent may never cross (test coverage must not drop, the API contract must not change), success criteria, and checkpoints that force it to report before decisive steps.

  file_access ∉ {/etc/*, ~/.ssh/*, config/secrets.yml}

  token_usage < 100k per task · response_time < 60s

          9.6 · Contract Net Protocol — multi-agent decision making
          

            
AIMA Chapter 18: A manager agent announces a task, contractor agents send bids, the manager selects the best contractor, and the contractor executes and reports back. Anthropic's multi-agent system and Cursor 2.0's parallel agents both use variants of this idea. It works well when tasks decompose cleanly; it backfires when sequential dependencies dominate.

            
A simplified user-level version: ask the agent, "If you split this task into 3 branches of work, what is the best split?" Then review the plan before execution and define an explicit rollback point if one branch fails.

          

        


        

          9.7 · Knowledge representation — anchoring context
          

            
AIMA Chapters 7 & 10: A logical agent reasons correctly only if the input facts are correct and the ontology is consistent. The quality of an agent's output is directly proportional to the quality and structure of the knowledge base you give it. The problem is often not that the agent is "stupid"; it is that the knowledge base you fed it is incomplete or contradictory.

            
Context anchoring for a regulated domain domain practitioner: documentation should define business ontology clearly. What is a "VIP customer"? What counts as a suspicious transaction? What does customer.status = 'A' really mean? Feed those definitions through RAG or CLAUDE.md. Stable ontology goes near the top of the context and benefits from caching; task-specific material goes near the bottom. Do not let the agent guess from generic training data, especially in sparse local domains.

          

        


        

          9.8 · Probabilistic reasoning — managing uncertainty
          

            
AIMA Chapters 12-17: The real world is not boolean. Three practical habits follow from this: (1) ask the agent for a confidence score and its assumptions; (2) think in terms of expected utility, not just expected correctness; (3) define fallback mechanisms when uncertainty is high.

            
Example: high-risk transaction classification with confidence ≥ 90% → the agent handles it automatically. Confidence 70-90% → flag for human review. Confidence < 70% → stop and request more context.

Constitutional AI (CAI) is the LLM training method Anthropic uses for Claude. It works in 2 stages:


  1. Self-critique: AI is given a "constitution" — a list of principles about truthfulness, harmlessness, and respect for users. AI rereads its own output, identifies which principles it violates, and revises itself.

  • Ask questions more effectively instead of trying to "break" the AI
  • Recognize that different AIs have different limits because they have different constitutions
  • Notice that Anthropic later expanded this into "Collective Constitutional AI" (FAccT 2024) to incorporate public input — a direction worth watching

Important: correct a common misunderstanding. MAD is not a "neuroscience measure" or a "neural signal jumping between distant nodes." MAD is an NLP method for scoring the novelty of text. It works by:


  1. Taking the word embedding (numeric vector) of the original word and the words in the answer
  2. Computing cosine distance between those vectors
  3. Taking the most distant word as the novelty score


Example: with the prompt "Think of uses for a brick," the answer "build a wall" has low distance (brick and wall are very close in embedding space). The answer "bird water tray" has much higher distance — the word "bird" is much farther from "brick" in semantic space.


Practical application: You can use the principle in a rough way during brainstorming. Ask yourself: "How tightly is my answer linked to the original keyword?" If it remains inside familiar combinations (small semantic distance), it is probably a safe idea — not a breakthrough. If it reaches into other knowledge domains (large distance), it may contain the seed of a breakthrough.


Critical limitation: Semantic distance measures novelty, not usefulness. A completely meaningless answer can have very high semantic distance. So: do not use this to judge final quality — only to detect whether your ideas are falling back into familiar grooves.


Note: Organisciak et al. (2023) showed that fine-tuned LLMs completely outperform semantic distance for creativity scoring. Meaning: Semantic Distance was an early step, but stronger tools now exist.

The Iowa Gambling Task is a classic experiment: participants choose cards from 4 decks, 2 "good" decks (small wins but sustainable), and 2 "bad" decks (big wins but even bigger losses).   • The "Prominent Deck B" phenomenon shows that healthy participants often prefer deck B (a bad deck) because it wins frequently, contrary to the theory's expectation
  • Dunn, Dalgleish & Lawrence (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews 2006) concluded that the evidence is "more mixed than supporters claim"


Practical implication: IGT and Somatic Markers are still useful for understanding that intuition has a biological basis — it is not superstition. But do not absolutize "listen to your body" as the only guide. In important decisions:


  • If you have a strong bodily reaction (tension, unease) even though logic says things are fine — pause and examine further. Your body may be detecting something your conscious mind has not yet articulated.
  • But do not turn intuition into "closed evidence" — it may also be bias or baseless fear.
